## Introduction

Social engineering attacks continue to rank as some of the most effective and insidious tactics used by adversaries in today's digital landscape. With the emergence of AI-driven phishing, deepfake impersonations, and the increasing adoption of IoT devices, attackers now have access to a broader array of tools to exploit human vulnerabilities.

To mitigate these threats, organizations must adopt a holistic approach to assessing and improving their defenses, with a particular emphasis on human factors. The **Social Engineering Testing (SET) Framework** is designed to simulate, test, and improve the resilience of employees and organizations against a wide range of social engineering tactics. Through automated attack simulations, real-time feedback, and continuous improvement cycles, organizations can enhance their preparedness and security awareness, ultimately fostering a culture of resilience.

---

## Overview of the SET Framework

### What is the SET Framework?

The SET Framework is a strategic methodology for assessing and addressing human vulnerabilities through simulated social engineering attacks. These attacks mimic real-world adversarial tactics, such as phishing, vishing, impersonation, and more recent threats driven by AI, such as deepfake-based impersonations and AI-generated phishing campaigns. By evaluating how employees respond to these threats, the SET Framework helps organizations generate actionable insights that directly inform improvements in security training and protocols.

### Core Components

<div className="component-grid">

**Scope Definition & Objective Setting**

The SET Framework begins with defining the scope of the simulation. This includes selecting specific departments, roles, and attack vectors to be tested. For example, high-risk areas like finance or human resources may be targeted for impersonation or spear-phishing tests, while broader groups might be exposed to generic phishing attacks. Setting clear objectives, such as improving awareness or reducing the click-through rate on phishing links, is critical for evaluating the effectiveness of subsequent interventions.

**Scenario Development & Customization**

Attack scenarios are designed based on the organization's specific risk profile and real-world threats. For instance, AI-driven spear-phishing emails can be tailored to simulate highly personalized attacks, whereas deepfake voice impersonations can target senior executives. Customizing these simulations ensures that the tests accurately reflect the evolving threat landscape and align with the organization's unique vulnerabilities.

**Automated Execution with AI Integration**

AI tools are employed to automate and scale the execution of various social engineering attacks. From simulating thousands of phishing emails to creating convincing deepfake impersonations, AI enables continuous and scalable testing. This integration also allows for adaptive attacks that can evolve in real time based on employee responses.

**Data Collection & Analysis**

Detailed metrics, such as how employees interacted with simulated attacks, are collected to assess vulnerabilities. For instance, metrics like the time-to-report (TTR) a suspicious email or the credential submission rate in phishing simulations can reveal critical weaknesses in employee awareness.

**Continuous Feedback, Reporting, & Improvement**

The SET Framework promotes continuous learning and improvement. Employees receive real-time feedback after simulations, helping them recognize mistakes and learn how to respond more effectively. Additionally, regular reports are generated to inform leadership of organizational progress, allowing for the iterative refinement of both testing scenarios and training programs.

## SET Tactics and Sub-Tactics

### SET-001: Pretexting

Pretexting is a sophisticated form of social engineering where attackers create elaborate, fabricated scenarios to trick victims into divulging sensitive information. It often involves impersonating a trusted individual or authority figure to exploit the victim's trust.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-001-01 | Impersonating Authority Figures | Attackers pretend to be executives, law enforcement officers, or other figures of authority, creating urgency or importance to manipulate the target |
| SET-001-02 | Creating Urgent Scenarios | Scenarios that create a sense of emergency, such as claiming a critical system is down and urgently requesting login credentials |
| SET-001-03 | Gaining Trust Through Familiarity | Pretending to know personal details about the target, such as referencing mutual contacts or past work projects |

### SET-002: Baiting

Baiting is a form of social engineering where attackers entice targets with promises of rewards, typically exploiting the target's curiosity or desire for a benefit.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-002-01 | Physical Media Drops | Leaving infected USB drives in visible locations such as parking lots or near office entrances |
| SET-002-02 | Online Baiting | Creating fake download links or software offers that promise valuable rewards |
| SET-002-03 | Scareware | Displaying fake security alerts urging users to download malicious software to "fix" non-existent problems |

### SET-003: Tailgating/Piggybacking

Tailgating, or piggybacking, occurs when unauthorized individuals gain physical access to restricted areas by exploiting the courtesy of authorized personnel.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-003-01 | Following Authorized Personnel | Gaining access by following an authorized employee through a secure door |
| SET-003-02 | Using Large Objects as Props | Carrying large objects to make it difficult for security personnel to question an individual |
| SET-003-03 | Pretending to Be an Employee | Impersonating employees or legitimate visitors with badges or uniforms |

### SET-004: Impersonation

Impersonation is the act of pretending to be someone trusted by the target, often exploiting the target's trust in authority figures or colleagues.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-004-01 | Phone Impersonation | Calling employees pretending to be executives, customers, or support personnel |
| SET-004-02 | In-Person Impersonation | Physically entering premises by pretending to be a contractor, IT support, or maintenance personnel |
| SET-004-03 | Email Impersonation | Business Email Compromise (BEC) attacks where attackers spoof trusted email addresses |

### SET-005: Quid Pro Quo

Quid Pro Quo attacks exploit the principle of reciprocity by offering something valuable in exchange for sensitive information.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-005-01 | Offering Free Services | Offering free tech support to gain access to sensitive data |
| SET-005-02 | Offering Rewards | Promising gift cards or special offers in exchange for personal information |
| SET-005-03 | Exploiting Curiosity | Offering information or services in exchange for seemingly harmless details |

### SET-006: Phishing

Phishing is one of the most widespread and effective social engineering tactics, involving deceptive emails to trick targets into revealing sensitive information.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-006-01 | Generic Email Phishing | Mass emails with general content designed to look legitimate from popular services |
| SET-006-02 | Spear Phishing | Targeted phishing with detailed information about a specific individual or organization |
| SET-006-03 | Clone Phishing | Copying legitimate emails from a trusted source with altered malicious links |

### SET-007: Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Vishing uses phone calls or voice messages to deceive individuals into divulging sensitive information.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-007-01 | Phone Calls from Fake Authorities | Calling pretending to be a bank representative, police officer, or company executive |
| SET-007-02 | Automated Voice Messages | Using automated messages to impersonate legitimate organizations with urgent claims |

### SET-008: Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Smishing uses SMS text messages to deceive targets into revealing personal information or clicking on malicious links.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-008-01 | Text Messages with Malicious Links | Fraudulent text messages from seemingly legitimate sources with phishing links |
| SET-008-02 | Fake Alerts and Notifications | Sending fake notifications claiming accounts are locked or information is at risk |

### SET-009: Dumpster Diving

Dumpster diving involves retrieving sensitive information that has been discarded in physical trash or digital media.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-009-01 | Searching for Discarded Information | Searching through office trash bins or dumpsters for sensitive documents |
| SET-009-02 | Recovering Discarded Devices | Recovering electronic devices that haven't been properly wiped |

### SET-010: Shoulder Surfing

Shoulder surfing involves physically observing individuals as they enter sensitive information.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-010-01 | Observing Password Entry | Watching over someone's shoulder as they enter login credentials or PINs |

### SET-011: Advanced Tactics

As technology evolves, social engineering tactics are becoming more sophisticated.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-011-01 | Watering Hole Attacks | Compromising legitimate websites frequently visited by target organization employees |
| SET-011-02 | Business Email Compromise (BEC) | Using phishing or malware to gain access to legitimate business email accounts for fraud |
| SET-011-03 | Physical Surveillance | Conducting physical surveillance to identify security weaknesses |
| SET-011-04 | Fake Job Offers | Creating fake job offers or LinkedIn connections to lure individuals |

Attackers exploit vulnerabilities in connected devices to manipulate employees or gain access to sensitive data.

| ID | Sub-Tactic | Description |
|----|------------|-------------|
| SET-018-01 | Smart Device Impersonation | Impersonating smart devices like virtual assistants or security systems |

## Assessment Methodology and Metrics

### Real-Time Vulnerability Assessments

Real-time vulnerability assessments simulate social engineering attacks in live environments, mirroring real-world conditions.

**Key Metrics:**

| Metric | Description | Target |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **Click-Through Rate (CTR)** | Percentage of employees who clicked on phishing links | < 5% |
| **Credential Submission Rate** | Number of employees who entered sensitive information | < 2% |
| **Time-to-Report (TTR)** | How quickly employees report suspicious activity | < 5 minutes |
| **Challenge Rate** | Percentage of employees who challenge unauthorized individuals | > 90% |

### Role-Based Vulnerability Assessments

Certain roles are particularly vulnerable to targeted social engineering attacks and require specialized assessment.

**Key Metrics:**

| Metric | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Role-Specific Attack Success Rate** | Success of simulated attacks targeted at specific roles |
| **Challenge Rate by Role** | How often individuals in high-risk roles challenge suspicious requests |
| **Role-Specific TTR** | Time taken by employees in specific roles to recognize and report attempts |

## Continuous Feedback, Training, and Improvement

### AI-Generated Insights

AI-driven tools provide advanced insights into employee responses to social engineering attacks.

**Key Metrics:**

| Metric | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Behavioral Risk Score (BRS)** | AI-generated score based on past employee behavior during simulations |
| **Anomaly Detection Rate (ADR)** | Percentage of anomalous behaviors detected during simulations |
| **Vulnerability Prediction Rate (VPR)** | Predicts which employees are most likely to fall for future attacks |
| **Adaptive Learning Progress (ALP)** | Tracks how quickly employees improve in recognizing attacks |

### Continuous Feedback Loops

#### Immediate Post-Test Feedback

After a simulated attack, employees receive instant feedback including:

- **Identification of Mistakes**: Specific red flags they missed
- **Tailored Learning Materials**: Training that addresses revealed vulnerabilities
- **Positive Reinforcement**: Recognition for successfully identifying and reporting attacks

#### Real-Time Reporting Encouragement

- **Automated Phishing Reporting Button**: One-click flagging of suspicious emails
- **Vishing Response Protocols**: Clear protocol for reporting suspicious phone calls
- **Immediate Response Alerts**: Automated alerts to security team when incidents are reported

### Benchmarking and Progress Tracking

| Benchmark | Goal | Purpose |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Phishing CTR | < 5% | Measure anti-phishing effort success |
| Time-to-Report | < 5 minutes | Assess awareness and response speed |
| Role-Specific Response | Lower than average | Ensure high-risk roles have stronger defenses |

## Advanced Metrics for Comprehensive Assessment

### Metrics for Testing Effectiveness

| Metric | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Attack Success Rate (ASR)** | Percentage of successful social engineering attacks during simulations |
| **Training Impact Rate (TIR)** | Compares performance before and after security awareness training |
| **Reporting Rate (RR)** | Percentage of employees who successfully report attempts |
| **Attack Progression Interruption Rate (APIR)** | Rate at which employees interrupt attack progression before goal completion |
